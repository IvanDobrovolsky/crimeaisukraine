# International Brands & Retail in Occupied Crimea

**Research date:** 2026-03-30
**Methodology:** Web research, secondary source analysis, investigative journalism review
**Scope:** Fashion, food, tech, automotive, logistics brands operating in or withdrawing from Crimea since 2014

---

## Executive Summary

After Russia's illegal annexation of Crimea in 2014, most major Western brands withdrew from the peninsula due to EU/US sanctions. However, several European companies continued operating through Russian subsidiaries, exploiting loopholes in sanctions enforcement. Key findings:

- **McDonald's** closed 3 Crimea stores in April 2014; **Burger King** initially planned to enter but reversed course
- **Metro (Germany)** and **Auchan (France)** continued operating supermarkets in Crimea despite EU sanctions
- **DHL (Germany)** maintained limited courier services in Crimea, investigated by Ukraine's SBU
- **Adidas** and **Puma** were investigated by Ukraine's SBU for possible operations in occupied Crimea (2018)
- **Siemens** turbines were illegally diverted to Crimea; criminal charges brought against 5 individuals in Germany (2024)
- **Xiaomi** briefly blocked phones in Crimea for sanctions compliance (2021), then reversed
- No evidence found that **Boggi Milano** opened stores in Crimea

## 9. Sanctions Circumvention -- Systematic Patterns

### 9.1 RFE/RL Investigation (2020)

A joint investigation by RFE/RL's Current Time, VOA, and Municipal Scanner found scores of European companies -- primarily offshore holding firms -- continued to operate in Crimea. Key findings:
- EU firms owned by tycoons invested in sectors prohibited by sanctions (tourism, transportation)
- Cyprus-registered firms controlled Crimean hotels
- Shares in one Cyprus firm controlling a Crimean hotel were held by a **British subsidiary of Bank of New York Mellon**

Source: [RFE/RL: The Crimea Circumvention](https://www.rferl.org/a/crimea-how-eu-firms-sidestepping-sanctions-making-money-on-peninsula/30303687.html)

### 9.2 OCCRP Investigation

- 600+ vessels entered Crimean ports over 2 years
- 24 vessels bearing EU country flags
- 43 vessels registered in the EU
- 22 vessels owned by EU businesses

Source: [OCCRP: International trade with Crimea ongoing despite sanctions](https://www.occrp.org/en/investigation/international-trade-with-crimea-ongoing-despite-sanctions)

### 9.3 Academic Research

The paper "Business As Usual: Sanctions Circumvention by Western Firms in Crimea" documents systematic patterns of sanctions evasion through corporate structures.

Source: [Academia.edu](https://www.academia.edu/40231189/Business_As_Usual_Sanctions_Circumvention_by_Western_Firms_in_Crimea)

## 12. Digital Question: Store Locators and Crimea Classification

### The Problem

When international brands maintain store locators on their websites, the classification of Crimea (listed under "Russia" vs. "Ukraine") is a form of digital sovereignty representation. This mirrors the map services question documented in `maps.md`.

### Current Status

Most brands with stores in Crimea have now exited, making this less testable. However:

- **Boggi Milano** (ru.boggi.com): Russian website lists stores by Russian city; Crimea cities not listed but would presumably fall under "Russia" if present
- **Brands that operated in Crimea via Russian subsidiaries** (Metro, Auchan, DHL): these stores were listed on Russian-language websites under Russian geographic classifications
- **TripAdvisor**: Lists Crimea under "Europe" without country designation (documented in travel.md)

### Methodological Note

Programmatic checking of store locators is theoretically possible (many use JSON APIs), but most relevant brands have now exited Russia entirely, making the question historical rather than current. The more productive digital audit targets are:
1. Map services (documented in `maps.md`)
2. Travel platforms (documented in `travel.md`)
3. Weather services (documented in `weather.md`)

## Key Findings for MFA Brief

1. **French and German companies were the worst offenders**: Metro (Germany), Auchan (France), Leroy Merlin (France), and DHL (Germany) all operated in occupied Crimea despite EU sanctions. Their legal defense -- operating via Russian subsidiaries -- exploited enforcement gaps.

2. **Sanctions enforcement has been weak**: Until the 2024 EU directive harmonizing criminal penalties, enforcement was inconsistent. The Siemens case (charges in 2024 for acts in 2015-2016) shows a 8-year lag between violation and prosecution.

3. **The "spirit vs. letter" gap**: EU officials acknowledged that Crimea operations by Metro and Auchan violated the "spirit" of sanctions, but the lack of a comprehensive trade embargo created legal gray areas.

4. **American companies were more compliant**: US firms (McDonald's, Visa, Mastercard, PayPal, Apple, Google) withdrew from Crimea promptly in 2014, likely due to stronger OFAC enforcement culture.

5. **Chinese companies filled the vacuum**: Post-2022, Chinese brands (especially Xiaomi) dominate the Russian consumer electronics market, with products reaching Crimea through Russian distribution.

6. **No evidence of Boggi Milano in Crimea**: Despite the user's suspicion, no evidence was found. Boggi Milano's Russia presence is limited to major mainland cities.

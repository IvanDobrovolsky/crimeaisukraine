# EU Legal & Policy Framework and Academic Sources for Crimea Digital Sovereignty Paper

**Compiled:** 2026-03-30
**Target journal:** Internet Policy Review

## 1. EU Council Decision 2014/145/CFSP (17 March 2014)

### Full Citation (APA 7)
Council of the European Union. (2014a). Council Decision 2014/145/CFSP of 17 March 2014 concerning restrictive measures in respect of actions undermining or threatening the territorial integrity, sovereignty and independence of Ukraine. *Official Journal of the European Union*, L 78, 16-21. https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32014D0145

### Key Content
- **Legal basis:** Article 29 of the Treaty on European Union.
- **Article 1:** Travel bans on natural persons "responsible for actions which undermine or threaten the territorial integrity, sovereignty and independence of Ukraine."
- **Article 2:** Asset freezes on listed persons and associated entities.
- Initially listed 21 individuals (Crimean officials, Russian military/political figures).
- Extended most recently until 15 September 2025 by Council Decision (CFSP) 2025/528.

### Does It Mention Maps/Digital Representation?
**No.** The Decision is entirely focused on individual sanctions (travel bans, asset freezes). It contains no provisions regarding maps, digital representation, geographic data, or platform content. This is a significant gap for the paper's argument.

### Connection to Crimea Paper
This Decision establishes the EU's political and legal position that Crimea's annexation is illegal and that actions undermining Ukraine's territorial integrity warrant sanctions. However, the sanctions regime's silence on digital/cartographic representation is itself a finding: the EU sanctions infrastructure targets physical persons and financial flows but completely overlooks the digital infrastructure through which territorial sovereignty is represented to billions of users daily.

## 2. EU Council Regulation (EU) No 692/2014 (23 June 2014)

### Full Citation (APA 7)
Council of the European Union. (2014b). Council Regulation (EU) No 692/2014 of 23 June 2014 concerning restrictive measures in response to the illegal annexation of Crimea and Sevastopol. *Official Journal of the European Union*, L 183, 9-14. https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2014/692/oj/eng

### Key Articles (Consolidated Version)
- **Article 2:** Prohibits import of goods originating in Crimea/Sevastopol; prohibits related financing, financial assistance, insurance, and reinsurance.
- **Article 2a** (added by amendment): Prohibits investment in Crimea/Sevastopol entities and real estate, including financing, joint ventures.
- **Article 2b:** Prohibits sale, supply, transfer, or export of goods and technology listed in Annex II for use in sectors: **transport, telecommunications, energy, and oil/gas/mineral extraction**. Also bans related technical assistance and brokering services.
- **Article 2c:** Prohibits technical assistance, brokering, construction, or engineering services directly relating to **infrastructure** in Crimea/Sevastopol in the sectors listed in Article 2b.
- **Article 2d:** Prohibits services directly related to **tourism activities** in Crimea/Sevastopol; bans cruise ships from calling at Crimean ports listed in Annex III.

### Does It Cover Geographic Data or Map Services?
**No.** The regulation explicitly covers physical goods (Annex II references Combined Nomenclature chapters 25-98: machinery, metals, electronics for industrial use) and specific sector services (transport, telecommunications infrastructure, energy, mining). "Telecommunications" in Article 2b refers to physical telecommunications infrastructure (cables, switching equipment), not informational or data services. Geographic data, mapping services, and digital platform content are not addressed.

### Connection to Crimea Paper
The regulation's mention of **telecommunications** infrastructure is the closest the EU sanctions regime comes to digital territory, but it refers to physical infrastructure, not informational content. This gap is analytically productive: the EU recognizes that physical infrastructure serving occupied Crimea requires restriction, but has not extended this logic to the digital/informational infrastructure that represents Crimea's sovereignty to global audiences. Article 2b's prohibition on technology for use in Crimea's telecommunications sector could theoretically be stretched to cover geographic data services, but no enforcement action has tested this interpretation.

### Related Decision
Council Decision 2014/386/CFSP (23 June 2014) is the CFSP counterpart to Regulation 692/2014, providing the political framework. https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32014D0386

## 3. EU Digital Services Act (Regulation 2022/2065)

### Full Citation (APA 7)
European Parliament & Council of the European Union. (2022). Regulation (EU) 2022/2065 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 19 October 2022 on a Single Market for Digital Services and amending Directive 2000/31/EC (Digital Services Act). *Official Journal of the European Union*, L 277, 1-102. https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2022/2065/oj/eng

### Provisions on Territorial Representation, Geographic Accuracy, Sovereignty
**The DSA contains no provisions specifically addressing territorial representation, geographic accuracy, or sovereignty in platform content.** Its focus is on illegal content moderation, transparency obligations, and systemic risk assessment for very large online platforms (VLOPs).

### Relevant Provisions for the Paper
- **Territorial scope (Article 2, Recitals 8-9):** Applies to intermediary services offered to recipients located in the EU, regardless of where the provider is established. A "substantial connection" to the EU is determined by establishment, user numbers, or targeting of activities (language, currency, domain).
- **Systemic risk assessment (Article 34):** VLOPs must identify and assess systemic risks, including "any actual or foreseeable negative effects on civic discourse and electoral processes, and public security." Misrepresentation of sovereign territory could potentially be framed under this provision, though it has not been tested.
- **Orders to act against illegal content (Articles 9-10):** National authorities can issue orders to platforms to act against specific illegal content, but the territorial effect is "in principle limited to the territory of the issuing Member State" unless the illegality derives from EU law.
- **Extraterritorial effect:** The DSA represents the EU's exercise of extraterritorial regulatory power over non-EU platforms, a practical assertion of digital sovereignty.

### Connection to Crimea Paper
The DSA is the EU's most comprehensive platform regulation, yet it has no mechanism for addressing how platforms represent territorial sovereignty. This is a critical regulatory gap. The paper can argue that Article 34's systemic risk assessment framework could be extended to cover cartographic misrepresentation of internationally recognized borders, particularly where such misrepresentation normalizes illegal annexation. The DSA's broad definition of "systemic risks" to civic discourse provides a potential hook, but one that requires active interpretation by the European Commission or Digital Services Coordinators.

## 4. EU Non-Recognition Policy: Legal Basis

### Key Legal Instruments
1. **UN General Assembly Resolution 68/262** (27 March 2014), "Territorial integrity of Ukraine" -- adopted by 100 votes in favour, 11 against, 58 abstentions. Affirms Ukraine's territorial integrity within internationally recognized borders; declares the Crimean referendum invalid. Non-binding but establishes international consensus.

2. **EU Council Conclusions** (March 2014 onward): The European Council has repeatedly stated that the EU "does not and will not recognise the illegal annexation of Crimea and Sevastopol by the Russian Federation."

3. **Council Decision 2014/145/CFSP and Regulation 692/2014** (above): Constitute the operational framework for the non-recognition policy.

### Legal Basis for Non-Recognition
The duty of non-recognition derives from:
- **Article 2 of the UN Charter:** Prohibition on the threat or use of force against the territorial integrity of states.
- **Customary international law:** The principle *ex iniuria ius non oritur* (unlawful acts cannot create legal rights) and the obligation *erga omnes* not to recognize situations created by serious breaches of peremptory norms (*jus cogens*).
- **ILC Articles on State Responsibility, Articles 40-41:** States shall not recognize as lawful a situation created by a serious breach of a peremptory norm, and shall cooperate to bring the breach to an end.

### Is There a Specific Obligation on EU-Based Companies?
**Not directly.** The non-recognition obligation in international law applies to *states*, not private companies. However:
- EU sanctions regulations (Regulation 692/2014) are binding on "natural or legal persons, entities or bodies... within the territory of the Union" (Article 8), creating derivative obligations on companies operating in EU jurisdiction.
- The specific sanctions do not require companies to represent Crimea as Ukrainian in their platforms; they only restrict specific economic activities.
- **This is a regulatory gap the paper can highlight**: while states are obligated not to recognize the annexation, no EU instrument translates this obligation into requirements for how digital platforms represent Crimean sovereignty.

### Key Academic Source
Milano, E. (2014). The non-recognition of Russia's annexation of Crimea: Three different legal approaches and one unanswered question. *Questions of International Law*, 1, 35-55. http://www.qil-qdi.org/the-non-recognition-of-russias-annexation-of-crimea-three-different-legal-approaches-and-one-unanswered-question/

Milano identifies three approaches: (1) **normativist** (non-recognition as automatic legal consequence of illegality); (2) **communitarian** (non-recognition required only for gross violations of peremptory norms, per ILC Articles 40-41); (3) **realist** (non-recognition as discretionary political tool). He raises the "unanswered question" of whether non-recognition will erode over time through the "normative Kraft des Faktischen" (normative power of facts). This directly supports the paper's argument that digital defaults create facts on the ground.

## 5. Pohle & Thiel (2020) -- "Digital Sovereignty"

### Full Citation (APA 7)
Pohle, J., & Thiel, T. (2020). Digital sovereignty. *Internet Policy Review*, 9(4), 1-19. https://doi.org/10.14763/2020.4.1532

### Key Arguments
Pohle and Thiel trace how 1990s "cyber exceptionalism" predicted the internet would render state sovereignty obsolete, but this prediction has inverted -- governments now actively reassert authority over digital spheres. They propose a **typology of three digital sovereignty claims**:

1. **State autonomy and infrastructure security:** Geographic restriction, national control over digital infrastructure and communications. Includes data localization, "Schengen Routing." Present in both authoritarian (China, Russia) and democratic states.
2. **Economic autonomy and competition:** Independence from foreign technology providers (especially US/Chinese). Industrial policy promoting domestic innovation (e.g., Gaia-X).
3. **User autonomy and individual self-determination:** Citizen capacity for conscious, independent action. Digital literacy, encryption protections. Primarily articulated in democratic countries.

### Key Quote
Digital sovereignty is understood "more as a discursive practice in politics and policy than as a legal or organisational concept." The authors warn that strengthening sovereignty through "infrastructures of control" may restrict fundamental rights.

### Connection to Crimea Paper
Pohle and Thiel's framework is directly applicable. Our findings reveal a **fourth dimension they do not address: territorial sovereignty in digital representation**. Their typology focuses on who controls infrastructure, data, and markets -- but not on how infrastructure *represents* sovereignty claims. When Natural Earth's SOVEREIGNT field labels Crimea as Russian, or when Google Maps draws a solid border for Russian users, these are exercises of what Pohle and Thiel would call "infrastructural power" but oriented toward territorial claims rather than the three categories they identify. We can argue that digital sovereignty must also encompass the question of *whose sovereignty is depicted* in default geographic datasets, not only who controls the infrastructure.

## 6. Couture & Toupin (2019) -- "What Does the Notion of 'Sovereignty' Mean When Referring to the Digital?"

### Full Citation (APA 7)
Couture, S., & Toupin, S. (2019). What does the notion of "sovereignty" mean when referring to the digital? *New Media & Society*, 21(10), 2305-2322. https://doi.org/10.1177/1461444819865984

### Key Arguments
Couture and Toupin analyze how "sovereignty" is mobilized by a diversity of actors -- from heads of state to indigenous scholars to anarchist-oriented tech collectives -- with very diverse conceptualizations. They argue the concept is used to promote goals as diverse as state protectionism, multistakeholder Internet governance, and protection against state surveillance. Their central finding is that significant portions of the literature cluster around a "digital sovereign of choice" and fail to engage across conceptual strands.

### Key Quote
The notion of sovereignty in the digital realm is "mobilized by a diversity of actors... with very diverse conceptualizations, to promote goals as diverse as state protectionism, multistakeholder Internet governance or protection against state surveillance."

### Connection to Crimea Paper
Couture and Toupin's emphasis on the *multiplicity of sovereignty claims* in digital spaces provides theoretical grounding for our argument. Crimea represents a case where competing sovereignty claims (Ukrainian de jure vs. Russian de facto) are *encoded* into digital infrastructure. Their framework helps explain why platforms adopt different representations for different audiences -- each representation reflects a particular sovereign claim. The paper can extend their analysis by showing that digital infrastructure does not merely *reflect* sovereignty disputes but actively *constitutes* them through default settings, API responses, and geocoding choices.

## 7. Other Internet Policy Review Articles

### Roberts et al. (2021)

**Full Citation (APA 7):**
Roberts, H., Cowls, J., Casolari, F., Morley, J., Taddeo, M., & Floridi, L. (2021). Safeguarding European values with digital sovereignty: An analysis of statements and policies. *Internet Policy Review*, 10(3), 1-28. https://doi.org/10.14763/2021.3.1575

**Key argument:** Roberts et al. define digital sovereignty as "a form of legitimate, controlling authority" over digital infrastructure, data, software, and standards. They analyze whether existing EU policies achieve the EU's stated digital sovereignty aims and find that data governance policies partially succeed, but measures to constrain large platforms and advance EU competitiveness fall short.

**Key quote:** The EU's digital sovereignty requires both "control" (ability to influence digital systems) and "legitimacy" (operating according to generally accepted principles with public consent through input, throughput, and output mechanisms).

**Connection to Crimea paper:** Roberts et al.'s framework of "legitimate controlling authority" can be applied to cartographic representation. If the EU claims digital sovereignty, that claim must extend to how internationally recognized territorial boundaries are represented in digital platforms operating within the EU. Their finding that EU policies are insufficient to constrain large platforms directly supports our argument about the regulatory gap in map representation.

### Lokot & Wijermars (2023)

**Full Citation (APA 7):**
Lokot, T., & Wijermars, M. (2023). The politics of internet freedom rankings. *Internet Policy Review*, 12(2), 1-22. https://doi.org/10.14763/2023.2.1710

**Key argument:** International rankings (particularly Freedom House's Freedom on the Net) actively define internet freedom and serve as "global pattern-setters" for how internet freedom is understood. These rankings function as tools of political and diplomatic influence rather than neutral measurement instruments.

**Connection to Crimea paper:** Lokot and Wijermars demonstrate that seemingly neutral measurement instruments embed political assumptions. This parallels our argument about map datasets: Natural Earth's SOVEREIGNT field, GDELT's geocoding, and Google Maps' border rendering all present themselves as neutral technical choices while embedding contested political positions. The parallel is methodologically useful -- if we accept that freedom rankings are political, we must equally accept that geographic "defaults" are political.

## 8. EU Crimea Platform and Digital Sovereignty

### Joint Declaration of the International Crimea Platform (23 August 2021)

**Key commitments:**
- Reaffirms "commitment to the sovereignty, political independence, unity and territorial integrity of Ukraine within its internationally recognized borders, extending to its territorial waters."
- Calls for maintaining pressure on Russia to end the occupation and restore Ukraine's control.
- Commits to protect human rights of ethnic Ukrainians and Crimean Tatars on the peninsula.
- Established the International Crimea Platform Expert Network.

### 2023 Joint Statement (23 August 2023)
Continued reaffirmation of territorial integrity and non-recognition.

### Does It Address Digital Sovereignty?
**No.** Neither the 2021 Declaration nor subsequent Joint Statements contain any specific commitments regarding digital sovereignty, digital platforms, maps, geographic data, or how technology companies represent Crimea. The Crimea Platform's focus is on diplomatic, human rights, and security dimensions of the occupation.

### Connection to Crimea Paper
The Crimea Platform's silence on digital representation is itself a finding. Despite 46 participating countries and annual summits, the international community has not addressed how the world's most-used digital platforms represent Crimean sovereignty. This reinforces the paper's argument that digital infrastructure operates as an invisible layer -- so embedded in everyday practice that even dedicated policy forums focused on Crimea fail to scrutinize it. The paper can recommend that the Crimea Platform adopt specific commitments on digital/cartographic representation.

## 9. Star & Ruhleder (1996) -- Infrastructure Theory

### Full Citation (APA 7)
Star, S. L., & Ruhleder, K. (1996). Steps toward an ecology of infrastructure: Design and access for large information spaces. *Information Systems Research*, 7(1), 111-134. https://doi.org/10.1287/isre.7.1.111

### Key Concepts (Eight Properties of Infrastructure)
1. **Embeddedness:** Infrastructure is "sunk into and inside of other structures, social arrangements, and technologies."
2. **Transparency:** Infrastructure "does not have to be reinvented each time or assembled for each task, but invisibly supports those tasks."
3. **Reach or scope:** Infrastructure reaches beyond a single event or site.
4. **Learned as part of membership:** New participants learn infrastructure through becoming members of a community of practice.
5. **Links with conventions of practice:** Infrastructure shapes and is shaped by conventions.
6. **Embodiment of standards:** Infrastructure is plugged into other infrastructures in standardized ways.
7. **Built on an installed base:** Infrastructure inherits strengths and limitations of its base.
8. **Becomes visible upon breakdown:** The "normally invisible quality of working infrastructure becomes visible when it breaks."

### Key Quote
Infrastructure is "a fundamentally relational concept" that "becomes infrastructure in relation to organized practices" -- it "appears only as a relational property, not as a thing stripped of use."

### Connection to Crimea Paper
Star and Ruhleder's framework is foundational for the paper's theoretical argument:
- **Transparency:** Natural Earth's SOVEREIGNT field is transparent in exactly this sense -- developers using the dataset do not interrogate its sovereignty assignments; they treat them as given infrastructure. When GDELT geocodes a Crimea-related news article as "Russia," the sovereignty assignment is invisible to the end user.
- **Embeddedness:** Geographic datasets are embedded in countless downstream applications (weather services, travel platforms, news dashboards, AI training data), multiplying the effect of any sovereignty assignment.
- **Becomes visible upon breakdown:** The political nature of geographic defaults only becomes visible when someone explicitly questions them -- as we do in this paper.
- **Built on installed base:** Natural Earth's choices propagate through the installed base of tools that depend on it, creating path dependency in sovereignty representation.

## 10. Winner (1980) -- "Do Artifacts Have Politics?"

### Full Citation (APA 7)
Winner, L. (1980). Do artifacts have politics? *Daedalus*, 109(1), 121-136. https://www.jstor.org/stable/20024652

### Key Arguments
Winner argues that technical artifacts can embody political arrangements in two ways:
1. **Designed to settle contests:** Technical systems are sometimes "ways of settling an issue in the affairs of a particular community" (p. 123) -- they can be deliberately designed to produce political outcomes (e.g., Robert Moses's low overpasses on Long Island preventing bus access).
2. **Inherently political technologies:** Some technologies are "strongly compatible with" or "strongly incompatible with" particular kinds of political relationships, regardless of designer intent (p. 131).

### Key Quote
"What matters is not technology itself, but the social or economic system in which it is embedded" -- yet Winner argues this view misses how artifacts *themselves* carry political weight.

### Connection to Crimea Paper (Natural Earth's SOVEREIGNT Field)
Winner's framework applies directly to Natural Earth's SOVEREIGNT field:
- The SOVEREIGNT field is a technical artifact that *settles a political contest* -- it assigns sovereign control over territory through a database attribute. When the field reads "Russia" for Crimea, the artifact has made a political determination that propagates through every application consuming the dataset.
- This is not necessarily deliberate political design (as with Moses's overpasses), but it is what Winner describes as an artifact that is "strongly compatible with" a particular political arrangement -- in this case, the normalization of Russia's annexation.
- The paper can argue that Winner's 1980 insight has gained new urgency in the era of geographic information systems: when a single database field can propagate a sovereignty determination to billions of API calls, the politics embedded in artifacts have exponentially greater reach than Moses's overpasses ever did.

## 11. Katz (2023) -- Google Maps and Quasi-Sovereign Power

### Full Citation (APA 7)
Katz, C. G. (2023). One map to rule them all: Google Maps and quasi-sovereign power in international legal disputes. *Hastings Science & Technology Law Journal*, 14(1), 67-108. https://repository.uclawsf.edu/hastings_science_technology_law_journal/vol14/iss1/4/

### Key Arguments
- Google Maps controls approximately 80% market share in digital mapping.
- Google has assumed "quasi-sovereign" authority over cartographic representation -- a function traditionally reserved for states.
- Google's internal process for resolving border and toponym disputes "lacks transparency, and is often at odds with the consensus of the international legal community."
- Google functions as "both an instigator and mediator of first instance in border and toponym disputes."
- Users and governments have granted Google quasi-sovereign legitimacy through the platform's ubiquity.

### Key Quote
Google's quasi-sovereign authority stems from its "practical dominance in cartographic data collection and display," granting the private company influence over states' "juridical functions" regarding territorial claims -- a role traditionally held exclusively by governments.

### Connection to Crimea Paper
Katz's analysis is directly on point. She provides the legal framework for understanding why Google Maps' representation of Crimea matters: when Google shows Crimea as Russian territory to Russian users (via geofencing), it is exercising quasi-sovereign power to validate an internationally illegal annexation. The paper can build on Katz by showing that this quasi-sovereign power extends beyond Google to the entire geographic data ecosystem (Natural Earth, GDELT, OpenStreetMap, Apple Maps, etc.), and by demonstrating empirically how different platforms exercise this power differently across the Crimea case.

## Summary of Regulatory Gaps (for the Paper's Argument)

| Instrument | Addresses physical/economic sanctions? | Addresses digital/cartographic representation? |
|---|---|---|
| Decision 2014/145/CFSP | Yes (travel bans, asset freezes) | No |
| Regulation 692/2014 | Yes (goods, services, investment, tourism, telecoms infrastructure) | No |
| Digital Services Act (2022/2065) | Partial (platform transparency, systemic risks) | No explicit provision |
| UNGA Res. 68/262 | Yes (affirms territorial integrity) | No |
| Crimea Platform Declarations | Yes (diplomatic commitments) | No |

**Core argument for the paper:** There is a complete regulatory vacuum in how digital platforms represent Crimean sovereignty. The EU has built an extensive sanctions architecture targeting economic activities in Crimea, but has not addressed the digital infrastructure through which territorial sovereignty is represented to global audiences. This gap means that the most visible, most frequently encountered representation of Crimea's status -- through map services, travel platforms, and geographic datasets used by billions -- operates entirely outside the EU's non-recognition framework.

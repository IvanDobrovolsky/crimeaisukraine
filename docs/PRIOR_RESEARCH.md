# Prior Research: How Digital Platforms Represent Crimea's Sovereignty

**Date compiled:** 2026-03-30

This document catalogs existing research, reports, investigations, and journalism that audit how digital platforms represent Crimea's sovereignty status. The goal is to assess what has already been published and identify gaps for a new systematic investigation.

---

## 1. MAP SERVICES

### 1.1 Journalism and News Coverage

**"Google Maps Displays Crimean Border Differently In Russia, U.S."**
- **Author/Org:** NPR (The Two-Way)
- **Date:** April 12, 2014
- **Platforms covered:** Google Maps
- **Key findings:** Google shows Crimea differently based on user location: solid line (Russian territory) in Russia, dashed line (disputed) on .com, provincial border in Ukraine. Google stated it follows "local legislation when displaying names and borders."
- **URL:** https://www.npr.org/sections/thetwo-way/2014/04/12/302337754/google-maps-displays-crimean-border-differently-in-russia-u-s

**"Apple and Google Maps accommodate Russia's annexation of Crimea"**
- **Author/Org:** TechCrunch (Natasha Lomas)
- **Date:** November 27, 2019
- **Platforms covered:** Apple Maps, Google Maps
- **Key findings:** Both Apple and Google Maps showed Crimea as Russian territory to users in Russia, following Kremlin pressure. Apple had previously refused to mark Crimea as belonging to any country.
- **URL:** https://techcrunch.com/2019/11/27/apple-and-google-maps-accommodate-russias-annexation-of-crimea/

**"Apple Maps now displays Crimea as part of Ukraine to viewers outside of Russia"**
- **Author/Org:** TechCrunch
- **Date:** March 4, 2022
- **Platforms covered:** Apple Maps
- **Key findings:** After Russia's full-scale invasion, Apple quietly changed its maps to show Crimea as part of Ukraine when viewed outside Russia. Previously, non-Russian users saw Crimea as belonging to no country or as disputed.
- **URL:** https://techcrunch.com/2022/03/04/apple-maps-now-displays-crimea-as-part-of-ukraine-to-viewers-outside-of-russia/

**"Why a Russian Invasion of Ukraine Would Be a Big Test for Google Maps"**
- **Author/Org:** TIME Magazine
- **Date:** February 2022
- **Platforms covered:** Google Maps
- **Key findings:** Examines how Google Maps has become a geopolitical actor, with decisions about border representation carrying significant weight. Notes that Google maintains 30+ localized map versions worldwide.
- **URL:** https://time.com/6148040/google-maps-influences-international-affairs/

**"Russia's war on Google and Apple Maps"**
- **Author/Org:** Slate
- **Date:** April 7, 2022
- **Platforms covered:** Google Maps, Apple Maps
- **Key findings:** Documents Russia's years-long information war on mobile mapping apps starting from 2014. Moscow pressured companies to show Crimea as Russian; after the 2022 invasion, Apple reversed its position for non-Russian users.
- **URL:** https://slate.com/technology/2022/04/russia-apple-google-maps.html

**"Crimea Is Ours: Russia Up In Arms After Google 'Incorrectly' Maps Peninsula"**
- **Author/Org:** RFE/RL
- **Date:** 2019
- **Platforms covered:** Google Maps
- **Key findings:** Russian outrage after Google briefly showed Crimea as Ukrainian to Russian users (described as a "technical mistake" by Google, later corrected).
- **URL:** https://www.rferl.org/a/google-russia-crimea-is-ours-russia/29759895.html

**"National Geographic maps Crimea as Russian: Maps can determine destiny"**
- **Author/Org:** Slate
- **Date:** March 2014
- **Platforms covered:** National Geographic
- **Key findings:** National Geographic announced it would show Crimea as part of Russia, following its "de facto" mapping policy. Sparked controversy about how authoritative map publishers shape perceptions of sovereignty.
- **URL:** https://slate.com/news-and-politics/2014/03/national-geographic-maps-crimea-as-russian-maps-can-determine-destiny.html

**"How Should Crimea Be Shown on National Geographic Maps?"**
- **Author/Org:** National Geographic
- **Date:** March 2014
- **Platforms covered:** National Geographic maps
- **Key findings:** NatGeo affirmed "Crimea is part of Ukraine" and rejected Russia's intervention, but stated its policy is to depict "de facto geographic situations." Critics note NatGeo shows Ukraine's borders in five different ways across products.
- **URL:** https://www.nationalgeographic.com/science/article/140319-national-geographic-maps-crimea-annexation-russia

### 1.2 The "Stop Mapaganda" Project (Most Systematic Audit Found)

**"Stop Mapaganda" -- 400-page report by Mykola Golubei (Holubei)**
- **Author/Org:** Mykola Golubei, independent researcher / "Stop Mapaganda" project
- **Date:** 2023-2024
- **Platforms covered:** Systematic audit of physical and digital map products across Europe. Examined publishers including Columbus Verlag, Rathgloben 1917, Kunth Verlag, Interkart, Franckh-Kosmos, Stiefel Eurocart, Mairdumont (Germany), Freytag-Berndt (Austria), National Geographic (USA), Tecnodidattica Spa, Edizioni White Star (Italy), Hallwag Kummerly+Frey (Switzerland).
- **Key findings:**
  - Up to 90% of educational products on sale in Germany depict Crimea as not part of Ukraine
  - Six different "occupation versions" of Ukraine's map exist in Western products
  - Some products already legitimize 2022 sham referendum results
  - Coined the term "mapaganda" -- conscious or unconscious misrepresentation of borders favoring the aggressor
- **Coverage:**
  - CEPA: https://cepa.org/article/behind-the-lines-mapaganda-and-the-cartographic-war-on-ukraine/
  - Ukrainer: https://www.ukrainer.net/en/en-stop-mapaganda/
  - VoxUkraine: https://voxukraine.org/en/mapaganda-or-how-russia-manipulates-maps
  - LIGA.net: https://www.liga.net/en/politics/articles/kak-ukrainets-nashel-v-evrope-desyatki-kart-s-rossiyskim-krymom-i-gotovit-iski-na-milliony
  - Detector Media: https://en.detector.media/post/russia-winning-the-battle-for-cartography-20-western-firms-produce-and-market-maps-depicting-ukraines-occupation

**NOTE:** This is the closest existing project to the one proposed. However, Golubei's work focuses primarily on physical cartographic products (globes, atlases, wall maps) sold in European bookstores, with some coverage of digital map services. It does NOT systematically audit digital platforms beyond maps (travel, social media, search, etc.).

### 1.3 OpenStreetMap

**OpenStreetMap WikiProject Crimea / Data Working Group Decision**
- **Author/Org:** OpenStreetMap Foundation Data Working Group
- **Date:** November 14, 2018 (decision); ongoing controversy
- **Platforms covered:** OpenStreetMap
- **Key findings:** OSM maps Crimea as part of Russia's administrative relation (de facto control), with boundaries marked as "disputed." This follows OSM's "ground truth" policy. The decision has been highly controversial, with ongoing edit wars and forum disputes.
- **URLs:**
  - https://wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/WikiProject_Crimea
  - https://osmfoundation.org/wiki/Working_Group_Minutes/DWG_2018-11-14_Crimea
  - https://euromaidanpress.com/2018/11/23/open-street-map-decides-to-mark-crimea-as-russian-territory/

### 1.4 Natural Earth / Geodata Defaults

**"How to move Crimea from Russia to Ukraine in maps with R"**
- **Author/Org:** Andrew Heiss (Georgia State University)
- **Date:** February 13, 2025
- **Platforms covered:** Natural Earth Vector data (used by thousands of data visualizations worldwide), GeoPandas, rnaturalearth
- **Key findings:** Natural Earth's default shapefiles show Crimea as Russian (de facto). Nearly two dozen GitHub issues exist about this. NE offers 31 country-specific "point of view" shapefiles as alternatives, but most developers use the default. This means most programmatic maps worldwide default to showing Crimea as Russian.
- **URL:** https://www.andrewheiss.com/blog/2025/02/13/natural-earth-crimea/
- **Related GitHub issues:**
  - https://github.com/nvkelso/natural-earth-vector/issues/391
  - https://github.com/geopandas/geopandas/issues/2382
  - https://github.com/ropensci/rnaturalearth/issues/116

## 2. ACADEMIC PAPERS

### 2.1 Legal/Policy Analysis

**"One Map to Rule Them All: Google Maps and Quasi-Sovereign Power in International Legal Disputes"**
- **Author/Org:** Catherine Grace Katz, UC Law SF Hastings Science & Technology Law Journal, Vol. 14, Issue 1
- **Date:** 2023
- **Platforms covered:** Google Maps (with 80% market share)
- **Key findings:** Google Maps exercises "quasi-sovereign power" by determining how borders appear worldwide. Examines Crimea, Arunachal Pradesh, and other disputed territories. Argues Google's localized maps can legitimize views at odds with international law.
- **URL:** https://repository.uclawsf.edu/hastings_science_technology_law_journal/vol14/iss1/4/

### 2.2 Internet Infrastructure Studies

**"The Internet in Crimea: a Case Study on Routing Interregnum"**
- **Author/Org:** Romain Fontugne (IIJ Research Lab), Ksenia Ermoshina (CNRS), Emile Aben (RIPE NCC)
- **Date:** 2020 (IFIP Networking Conference)
- **Platforms covered:** BGP routing, AS dependencies, ISP infrastructure in Crimea
- **Key findings:** Russian control of Crimean internet infrastructure followed a "soft substitution" model taking about three years. Rostelecom deployed a submarine cable (Kerch Strait Cable) and created Miranda Media as local agent. Documented the transition from Ukrainian to Russian internet infrastructure.
- **URLs:**
  - https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/9142776
  - https://hal.science/hal-03100247/document

**"Measuring the Fragmentation of the Internet: The Case of the Border Gateway Protocol (BGP) During the Ukrainian Crisis"**
- **Author/Org:** Frederic Douzet, Louis Petiniaud, Loqman Salamatian, Kevin Limonier, Kave Salamatian, Thibaut Alchus
- **Date:** 2020 (NATO CyCon -- 12th International Conference on Cyber Conflict)
- **Platforms covered:** BGP routing infrastructure
- **Key findings:** Russian authorities and separatist forces modified BGP routes to divert local internet traffic from continental Ukraine, drawing a "digital frontline" consistent with the military one. Developed methodology to define and map digital borders at the routing level.
- **URL:** https://ccdcoe.org/uploads/2020/05/CyCon_2020_9_Douzet_Petiniaud_Salamatian_Limonier_Salamatian_Alchus.pdf

**"The Russification of Ukrainian IP Registration"**
- **Author/Org:** Kentik (Doug Madory)
- **Date:** 2022
- **Platforms covered:** IP address registration (RIPE NCC), geolocation databases
- **Key findings:** IP registrations in Crimea were changed from Ukraine to Russia -- some on the exact day of annexation (March 18, 2014). Creates an "impossible mix" of country-level geolocation data. Ukrainians using IPs suddenly classified as Russian face geoblocking of services sanctioned against Russia.
- **URL:** https://www.kentik.com/blog/the-russification-of-ukrainian-ip-registration/

### 2.3 Journalism and Information Control

**"Voices from the Island: Informational annexation of Crimea and transformations of journalistic practices"**
- **Author/Org:** Ksenia Ermoshina, published in Journalism (SAGE), Vol. 25(3), 528-546
- **Date:** 2023 (published); 2024 (journal issue)
- **Platforms covered:** Telecommunications infrastructure, broadcasting, digital security practices
- **Key findings:** Documents the progressive transition of telecom/broadcasting infrastructure under Russian influence after 2014. Between 2014-2015, dozens of Ukrainian media organizations and independent journalists left Crimea. Analyzes how "informational annexation" challenges journalistic work on infrastructural and organizational levels.
- **URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/14648849231152359

### 2.4 Digital Cartography Theory

**"When the Map Lies: How Digital Cartography Shapes Territorial Claims"**
- **Author/Org:** GNL UCPIL
- **Date:** Recent (undated)
- **Platforms covered:** Google Maps, Apple Maps, Bing Maps (general analysis)
- **Key findings:** Digital mapping companies conduct "private diplomacy" -- negotiating borders through interface design rather than treaties. Platforms have become "de facto territorial gatekeepers." The same territory appears Russian in Moscow and Ukrainian elsewhere, normalizing territorial claims.
- **URL:** https://www.gnlucpil.com/post/when-the-map-lies-how-digital-cartography-shapes-territorial-claims

## 3. THINK TANK AND NGO REPORTS

### 3.1 CEPA (Center for European Policy Analysis)

**"Behind the Lines: 'Mapaganda' and the Cartographic War on Ukraine"**
- **Date:** 2023
- **Platforms covered:** Maps, globes, atlases by Western publishers
- **Key findings:** Documented the "mapaganda" phenomenon. Notable example: the International Olympic Committee apologized after Crimea was separated from Ukraine on the Olympic Games website (2021). French and Slovenian TV showed maps with Crimea as Russian.
- **URL:** https://cepa.org/article/behind-the-lines-mapaganda-and-the-cartographic-war-on-ukraine/

### 3.2 Amnesty International

**"Russia/Ukraine: 10 Years of Occupation of Crimea"**
- **Date:** March 18, 2024
- **Platforms covered:** Not digital platform-focused; covers education, religion, media, demographics
- **Key findings:** As early as March 2014, Ukrainian TV/radio channels were taken off air and replaced by Russian media. 9% of children were taught in Ukrainian pre-annexation; now less than 1%. Extensive documentation of identity suppression. Not focused on how international digital platforms represent Crimea.
- **URL:** https://www.amnesty.org/en/documents/eur50/7805/2024/en/

### 3.3 EUvsDisinfo

**"Russia's Information Grip on Ukraine's Occupied Territories"**
- **Date:** 2025
- **Platforms covered:** Telecommunications, messaging apps (Telegram, WhatsApp, Instagram)
- **Key findings:** 5-6 million Ukrainians in occupied territories cut off from Ukrainian media. Miranda Media became dominant provider. Since August/September 2025, Crimea experienced widespread disruptions to Telegram, WhatsApp, and Instagram.
- **URL:** https://euvsdisinfo.eu/russias-information-grip-on-ukraines-occupied-territories/

### 3.4 CyberPeace Institute

**"War in Ukraine: The Struggle for Computer Network Control and its Impact on Civilians"**
- **Date:** 2022-2023 (quarterly reports)
- **Platforms covered:** Internet infrastructure, ISPs, network routing
- **Key findings:** Since 2014, Russia has systematically sought to sever Ukrainian networks in occupied regions. In cities like Kherson, Russian forces took over telecom facilities, introduced Russian ISPs, forced citizens to switch to SIM cards requiring Russian ID. Documented 2,000+ conflict-linked cyberattacks across 50+ countries.
- **URL:** https://cyberpeaceinstitute.org/publications/war-in-ukraine-computer-network-control-and-impact-on-civilians/

### 3.5 Harvard -- Russia in Global Perspective

**"Crimea Map" exhibit from "Google, Russian Sovereignty, and Algorithmic Authority"**
- **Author/Org:** Jenny Shore, Harvard University (Russia in Global Perspective project)
- **Date:** Undated (academic exhibit)
- **Platforms covered:** Google Maps
- **Key findings:** After Putin signed the treaty annexing Crimea, Google faced a dilemma and ultimately showed Crimea as part of Russia only to Russian users. Argues this demonstrates "a new layer of authority in the digital age that supersedes that of governments and laws."
- **URL:** https://russiaglobal.omeka.fas.harvard.edu/exhibits/show/google/jenny-shore/crimeamap

### 3.6 Access Now

**"Updates: Digital rights in the Russia-Ukraine conflict"**
- **Date:** Ongoing since 2022
- **Platforms covered:** Multiple digital platforms, internet access
- **Key findings:** Broad documentation of digital rights impacts across the conflict, including internet shutdowns, platform restrictions, and censorship.
- **URL:** https://www.accessnow.org/digital-rights-ukraine-russia-conflict/

## 7. GAP ANALYSIS: What Has NOT Been Done

Based on this comprehensive search, the following gaps exist in existing research:

### 7.1 No Cross-Platform Systematic Audit
No single study has systematically audited how Crimea is represented across ALL major digital platform categories (maps, travel, social media, search engines, reference sites, tech infrastructure) simultaneously. Golubei's "Stop Mapaganda" comes closest but focuses on physical cartographic products and maps specifically.

### 7.2 Travel Platform Sovereignty Metadata
No one has systematically checked how travel platforms (Booking.com, Airbnb, Expedia, Google Flights, Skyscanner, etc.) categorize Crimea in their country fields, search filters, and property metadata -- separate from sanctions compliance.

### 7.3 Social Media Location Systems
No audit examines how Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, X/Twitter, Snapchat, etc. classify Crimea in their location tagging, check-in, and geotagging systems.

### 7.4 Search Engine Knowledge Panels
No study documents what Google, Bing, DuckDuckGo, and Yandex show in knowledge panels/info boxes when users search "Crimea" -- specifically what country is listed.

### 7.5 Current State (Post-2022)
Most coverage of map services is from 2014 or 2019. The 2022 full-scale invasion caused some platforms to change their approach (Apple Maps, notably). A fresh audit of the current state across all platforms would be timely.

### 7.6 Quantified Impact
No study has quantified how many daily users are exposed to incorrect representations, or measured the reach/impact of different platform choices.

### 7.7 Tech Infrastructure Beyond Routing
While BGP routing and IP geolocation have been studied, no audit examines how CDNs, DNS, cloud providers, domain registrars, and other infrastructure classify Crimea.

---

## 8. ASSESSMENT OF NOVELTY

A new project systematically auditing digital platforms' representation of Crimea would be **highly novel** if it:

1. **Goes cross-platform** -- covering maps, travel, social media, search, reference, and infrastructure in one study
2. **Provides current-state snapshots** -- much existing work is from 2014 or 2019
3. **Includes travel and social media platforms** -- almost entirely unexamined
4. **Documents exact URLs/paths** for reproducibility
5. **Creates a categorized scorecard** (correct/ambiguous/incorrect per platform)
6. **Quantifies user exposure** -- how many people see what

The "Stop Mapaganda" project by Golubei is the most directly comparable prior work but is focused on physical maps in European bookstores and a narrower set of digital map services, not the broader digital platform ecosystem.

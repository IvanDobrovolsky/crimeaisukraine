# Published Materials Audit: Crimea Sovereignty Classification

**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Scope:** Atlases, textbooks, encyclopedias, travel guides, academic publications, educational materials
**Methodology:** Web research, cross-referencing Stop Mapaganda project findings, publisher catalogs, news reports

---

## 1. Commercial Atlases and Map Products

### 1.1 German Educational Map Products

The **Stop Mapaganda** project by **Mykola Holubei (Golubei)** documented that **up to 90% of German educational map products** on sale -- school textbooks, atlases, globes, wall maps, tourist products -- depict Crimea and other temporarily occupied territories as not part of Ukraine. His 400-page report identified over 30 internationally respected companies across Germany, Switzerland, Austria, Great Britain, Italy, and the United States distributing maps with incorrect Ukrainian borders.

#### Diercke Weltatlas (Westermann Verlag)
- **Title:** Diercke Weltatlas, Ausgabe 2023 (ISBN 978-3-14-100900-2)
- **Publisher:** Westermann Gruppe, Germany
- **Crimea classification:** **Inconsistent** -- Westermann marks Crimea in various ways across its products: as a "disputed territory," as part of the Russian Federation, or as an independent state unit separated from Ukraine
- **Audience:** Germany's most widely used school atlas; used in virtually all German federal states. Estimated 10+ million copies in circulation across editions
- **Digital version:** Diercke Atlas App (2025 edition available)
- **Fixable:** Westermann has a dedicated Ukraine resources page (diercke.de/ukraine) with free Ukrainian-language map packages, suggesting awareness of the issue, but core atlas products reportedly still use ambiguous boundary markings
- **Status:** Needs independent verification of 2023/2025 edition maps

#### MairDumont Group (Marco Polo, Falk, ADAC, Kunth)
- **Publisher:** MairDumont GmbH & Co. KG, Ostfildern, Germany -- country's largest travel publishing group
- **Products identified:**

| Brand | Product | Crimea Classification | Notes |
|-------|---------|----------------------|-------|
| **Falk** | World/European atlases | Showed 5 Ukrainian regions with "disputed state border" | BBC investigation confirmed |
| **ADAC** | Motoring atlases | Crimea, Donetsk, Tavria, Zaporizhzhia depicted as part of Russia | Motorist atlas with wide distribution |
| **Marco Polo** | World atlas (2023) | 5 regions separated by dotted line as "contested state border" | Purchased at Stanfords, London |
| **Kunth** | Atlas (2023 edition) | Separated Zaporizhzhia, Kherson, Donetsk, Luhansk, Crimea with dotted "contested" border | Published 2023 |

- **Publisher response:** MairDumont claims it "recognises all five regions as the territory of Ukraine" but uses dotted lines to denote "de facto conflict zone." Experts rejected this, noting that if the intent were a safety warning, the entire territory of Ukraine should be marked as a war zone
- **Audience:** Marco Polo produces over half of all world atlases sold in German schools
- **Fixable:** Publisher aware of criticism; no confirmed correction as of research date

#### Klett-Perthes (Haack Weltatlas)
- **Publisher:** Ernst Klett Verlag, Stuttgart (acquired Justus Perthes cartographic heritage in 1992)
- **Products:** Haack Weltatlas, Klett-Perthes World Atlas, classroom wall maps, digital atlas
- **Crimea classification:** Not independently confirmed in current edition; historically a major German school atlas publisher
- **Audience:** Widely used in German schools, particularly in eastern German federal states
- **Fixable:** Unknown -- requires direct examination of current edition

### 1.3 American Atlases

#### National Geographic Atlas of the World
- **Publisher:** National Geographic Society
- **Crimea classification:** **Crimea shown as part of the Russian Federation, "claimed" by Ukraine**
- **Policy:** National Geographic maps "incorporate a policy of depicting de facto geographic situations" -- i.e., states of affairs existing in fact or reality. This means they show Russian control as the baseline and Ukrainian sovereignty as a "claim"
- **Controversy:** In March 2014, National Geographic announced it would make Crimea part of Russia in its world map. The Stop Mapaganda project found National Geographic marks Ukraine's borders in **five different ways** across its products, ranging from "Crimea as an integral part of Ukraine" to "Crimea as a territory of Russia, claimed by Ukraine"
- **Audience:** Extremely high -- National Geographic brand has global recognition; atlas used in schools and libraries worldwide
- **Fixable:** National Geographic has been aware of criticism since 2014 but maintains its "de facto" policy. The framing that Ukraine is "claiming" its own internationally recognized territory effectively inverts the legal reality and presents the aggressor's position as the default
- **Status:** As of most recent examination, Crimea shown as Russian territory "claimed by" Ukraine

#### Oxford Atlas of the World
- **Title:** Oxford Atlas of the World, 26th Edition (2024)
- **Publisher:** Oxford University Press (in association with Philip's)
- **ISBN:** 978-0-19-769742-9
- **Crimea classification:** Not independently confirmed for 2024 edition; updated annually
- **Audience:** Considered a gold-standard reference atlas; used in academic and library settings
- **Fixable:** Annual updates make this highly correctable

#### Rand McNally
- **Publisher:** Rand McNally, Chicago
- **Products:** Road Atlas, World Atlas (2024 edition -- renamed and repackaged, same contents)
- **Crimea classification:** Not independently confirmed; Rand McNally has not been specifically cited in major Crimea mapping controversies
- **Audience:** Dominant US road atlas publisher; world atlas has smaller market share
- **Status:** Requires direct examination

### 1.4 Chinese Atlases

#### Zhongguo Ditu Chubanshe (China Cartographic Publishing House)
- **Publisher:** China Cartographic Publishing House (state-owned), under China's Ministry of Natural Resources
- **Products:** Shijie Ditu Ji (Collection of Maps of the World), various world and regional atlases
- **Crimea classification:** **Likely shows Crimea as Russian territory** (de facto alignment), though China officially abstained from the 2014 UN vote and does not formally recognize Russia's annexation of Crimea
- **Context:** China's state-owned Standard Map Service released 2023 maps that showed Russian territory (Bolshoy Ussuriysky Island) as Chinese, demonstrating willingness to deviate from other countries' claimed borders. China's maps are centrally controlled by the Ministry of Natural Resources -- all published maps must conform to state standards
- **Audience:** All Chinese-published atlases, used by 1.4 billion people domestically; also exported
- **Fixable:** Only through Chinese government policy change; maps are state-controlled
- **Status:** Requires direct examination of current Chinese world atlas edition

## 2. Textbooks

### 2.1 The OUP Geog.3 Incident (Key Case Study)

- **Title:** geog.3 Student Book (4th Edition)
- **Author:** RoseMarie Gallagher, Richard Parish
- **Publisher:** Oxford University Press
- **Year:** 2015 (original); corrected edition February 29, 2016
- **ISBN:** 978-0-19-839304-7
- **Target audience:** UK Key Stage 3 students (ages 11-14)
- **What happened:**
  - Original edition stated Russia "took [Crimea] from Ukraine in 2014"
  - Showed Crimea in a different color from Ukraine on maps
  - Noted citizens "voted for it to become part of Russia"
  - **No mention of illegality, international condemnation, or UN position**
- **Response:**
  - Ukrainian Embassy in London formally protested
  - OUP withdrew the original from sale (October 2015)
  - Corrected edition released February 2016
  - New text states "under international law Crimea is part of Ukraine" and that "the United Nations does not accept that Crimea is a Russian territory"
  - OUP offered free exchanges of erroneous copies
- **Reach:** Widely used across UK schools; geog. series is one of the most popular KS3 geography textbooks
- **Current status:** Corrected -- but unknown number of original copies still in classroom use

### 2.2 University Textbooks

#### A Geography of Russia and Its Neighbors (Guilford Press)
- **Title:** A Geography of Russia and Its Neighbors, Second Edition
- **Author:** Mikhail S. Blinnikov (St. Cloud State University)
- **Publisher:** Guilford Press (US edition) / Routledge (international)
- **ISBN:** 978-1-4625-4461-5 (hardcover), 978-1-4625-4459-2 (paperback)
- **Crimea classification:** Includes chapter titled **"Ukraine and Crimea: Together or Not?"** -- frames Crimea annexation as an event to be analyzed, covers events through 2019. Described as "thoroughly revised with current data and timely topics, such as the annexation of Crimea and Sevastopol"
- **Assessment:** The chapter title framing ("Together or Not?") presents what should be a settled legal question as an open one. However, the book is described as an academic geography text, not a political advocacy piece. Author is Russian-born, US-based academic
- **Audience:** Standard undergraduate textbook on Russian geography; adopted across US university courses
- **Fixable:** Third edition could update framing

#### World Regional Geography (Open Textbook)
- **Title:** World Regional Geography (open-access pressbook)
- **Publisher:** Pressbooks (open educational resource)
- **Crimea coverage:** Russia chapter includes Crimea discussion; accessible at pressbooks.pub/worldgeo/chapter/russia/
- **Assessment:** Placing Crimea within a "Russia" chapter rather than a "Ukraine" chapter implicitly classifies it as part of Russia's geography
- **Audience:** Used as free/open textbook across US community colleges and universities

### 2.3 Russian School Textbooks (State-Mandated)

#### New Russian History Textbook (2023)
- **Title:** History textbook for Russian schools (new unified edition, 2023)
- **Authors:** State-commissioned; announced by Education Minister Sergey Kravtsov
- **Publisher:** Russian Ministry of Education
- **Crimea classification:** **Crimea shown as integral part of Russian Federation** ("Reunification with Crimea" is a lesson topic)
- **Content:** Includes sections on "reasons for the start of the special military operation," "denazification," "demilitarization," and "entry of new regions into the Russian Federation." Maps show all occupied Ukrainian regions as Russian territory
- **Distribution:** Distributed across all of Russia AND in occupied Ukrainian territories (Donbas, Zaporizhzhia, Kherson)
- **Condemnation:** Amnesty International called it "a blatant attempt to unlawfully indoctrinate school children" (September 2023). VOA reported it "teaches lies to students in occupied Ukraine." CNN covered it as "Russia's latest effort to sway young minds"
- **Audience:** All Russian schoolchildren (~15 million) plus children in occupied territories
- **Fixable:** Only through regime change or policy reversal in Russia

### 2.4 AP / IB Geography Materials

- **AP Human Geography:** The College Board's AP Human Geography curriculum uses Russia's annexation of Crimea as a case study for **irredentism** -- i.e., a state's claim to territory in another country based on ethnic or historical ties. This framing correctly treats the annexation as a political action to be analyzed, not a legitimate border change. Crimea is not shown as Russian territory in AP materials
- **IB Geography:** No specific evidence found of IB materials classifying Crimea as Russian territory
- **Status:** AP/IB frameworks appear to handle this correctly

## 5. Encyclopedias

### 5.1 Encyclopaedia Britannica

- **Publisher:** Encyclopaedia Britannica, Inc.
- **Crimea entry:** Describes Crimea as **"an autonomous republic in southern Ukraine"**
- **Key language:** States that "in 2014 Russia covertly invaded and illegally annexed Crimea, a move that was denounced by the international community." References the 1997 Treaty of Friendship, Cooperation, and Partnership affirming Crimea as Ukrainian territory
- **Classification:** **Crimea is Ukrainian territory, illegally occupied by Russia**
- **Print status:** Britannica ceased print publication in 2012 after 244 years. The final print edition (2010, 15th edition) predates the annexation. All current content is digital-only (britannica.com), updated continuously (most recent update: March 2026)
- **Audience:** Global; Britannica.com is one of the world's most-visited reference sites
- **Assessment:** Britannica's framing is **correct** and uses strong language ("illegally annexed," "covertly invaded")

### 5.2 World Book Encyclopedia

- **Publisher:** World Book, Inc. (subsidiary of Berkshire Hathaway)
- **Print status:** World Book continues to publish annual print editions (one of the last major encyclopedias to do so)
- **Crimea classification:** Not independently confirmed for the most recent edition; World Book's editorial approach to disputed territories has not been the subject of public controversy
- **Audience:** Primarily US school and public libraries; children and students
- **Fixable:** Updated annually
- **Status:** Requires direct examination of current print edition

### 5.3 Bolshaya Rossiyskaya Entsiklopediya (Great Russian Encyclopedia)

- **Publisher:** Russian government (state-funded project)

## 9. Key Findings

### 9.1 The German Problem Is the Most Severe

Germany is the epicenter of incorrect Crimea cartography in the Western world. The MairDumont group alone (Marco Polo, Falk, ADAC, Kunth) produces a majority of school atlases sold in Germany and has been documented showing Crimea incorrectly. Westermann (Diercke) is inconsistent. This means that the vast majority of German students are learning geography from materials that show Crimea as separated from or not part of Ukraine.

### 9.2 National Geographic's "De Facto" Policy Is the Most Consequential Single Decision

National Geographic's policy of showing "de facto" control rather than internationally recognized borders means that a globally recognized and trusted brand effectively legitimizes Russia's annexation. The framing of Ukraine as "claiming" its own internationally recognized territory inverts the legal reality. Given National Geographic's reach in education, this is arguably the single most impactful incorrect representation.

### 9.3 The OUP Correction Is a Replicable Model

The successful correction of OUP's geog.3 textbook -- through Ukrainian Embassy pressure, public outcry, withdrawal, correction, and free exchange -- provides a template for addressing other publishers. Key elements: formal diplomatic protest, media coverage, publisher willingness to correct, free replacement of erroneous copies.

### 9.4 Travel Guides Are Correct but Obsolete

Major Western travel publishers (Lonely Planet, Bradt) correctly included Crimea in their Ukraine guides, but no new editions have been published since 2022. The absence of updated guides means there is no current authoritative Western travel reference for Crimea at all.

### 9.5 Russian State Publications Are Systematically Incorrect and Unfixable

Russian school textbooks, encyclopedias, and travel guides uniformly treat Crimea as Russian territory. The 2023 unified history textbook extends this indoctrination to occupied Ukrainian territories. These are state-controlled and cannot be changed through external pressure.

### 9.6 Gaps in the Research

The following require direct physical examination and cannot be confirmed through web research alone:
- Current editions of Rand McNally atlases
- Philip's Atlas of the World
- Collins World Atlas (current Crimea depiction specifically)
- Chinese-published world atlases
- Pearson, McGraw-Hill, Cambridge University Press geography textbooks
- World Book Encyclopedia current print edition
- Specific globe models from Replogle, Columbus, and other manufacturers
- AP/IB Geography textbook maps (as opposed to curriculum frameworks)

## 10. Recommendations for Research Paper

1. **Prioritize Germany:** The MairDumont/Westermann complex represents the largest documented source of incorrect Crimea cartography in the West. A section on German educational materials could be the paper's strongest empirical contribution.

2. **National Geographic deserves its own section:** The "de facto" policy and its implications for educational legitimacy warrant detailed analysis.

3. **Use the OUP case as a success story:** The geog.3 correction demonstrates that publisher engagement works.

4. **Commission physical verification:** Several key publications need hands-on examination. Consider purchasing current editions of the top 10 atlases for direct comparison.

5. **Cross-reference with Stop Mapaganda's 400-page report:** Mykola Holubei's full report likely contains ISBN-level detail not available through web research. Contact him directly for collaboration or data sharing.
